Understanding Net Profit: How to Calculate Your Business Margins

Learn the difference between revenue, gross profit, and net profit, and discover what a "healthy" margin looks like for your industry.

Toledah
March 14, 2026

What is Net Profit?

Three terms that always show up in the business world are Revenue, Gross Profit, and Net Profit. Understanding the transition from one to the other is vital for any business owner looking to scale sustainably.

Figure 1: The Profit Funnel — How earnings filter down after costs and expenses.

Revenue is simply the sum of all sales made by a business. It's the "top line" figure before anything is taken out.

Gross Profit is the revenue less the cost of goods sold (COGS). This represents what’s left after paying for the actual products or raw materials you sold.

Example:
Revenue: €5,400
Cost of Goods: €4,800
Gross Profit: €5,400 - €4,800 = €600

The Net Profit is the gross profit minus all expenses incurred to make those sales. This includes "operating expenses" like rent, electricity, marketing, and staff wages.

Continuing the example:
Labour: €100 | Energy: €55 | Servers: €45
Total Expenses: €200

Net Profit: €600 - €200 = €400

Calculating the Margin

The Net Profit Margin reveals how much of every euro earned actually remains as profit. It is calculated as:

The Formula
(Net Profit / Revenue) × 100
(€400 / €5,400) × 100 ≈ 7.41%

What is a Healthy Net Profit Margin?

The definition of a healthy net profit margin depends heavily on industry, business model, and location. A businessman comparing their pharmacy to a SaaS company would yield misleading results.

Industry / Business Type Typical Net Profit Margin
Large e-retail chains (Amazon, Walmart) 2–6%
Commodity distribution businesses 1–3%
Software / SaaS businesses 20–40%
💡 Pro Tip

Track net profit margins monthly. Watching trends over time is more valuable than one-off calculations. If margins are shrinking while revenue grows, expenses may be spiraling out of control.

Key Takeaways

  • ✓ Revenue: Total sales made.
  • ✓ Gross Profit: Revenue minus cost of goods sold.
  • ✓ Net Profit: Gross profit minus all operating expenses.
  • ✓ Healthy Margins: Vary by industry; low-margin operations are not necessarily failing.
